# When AI Starts Spending Money

A friend asked me the other day: What's all this optimization for?

I said, to get AI to mention me when it answers questions.

He smiled and said, that idea's already outdated.

I froze. Outdated?

He said, what you're thinking about is GEO—Generative Engine Optimization. Getting ChatGPT to mention your name, getting Claude to cite your articles. Sounds nice, but this approach has a ceiling.

What ceiling?

He asked me a question: What if AI could not just mention you, but directly call your service?

---

I went home and thought about it all night.

GEO's logic is exposure. User asks a question, AI answers, your name appears in the answer. User sees it, searches for you, finds you.

That's still humans looking for information.

But AIO's logic is invocation. User asks a question, AI thinks your service can solve it, and calls it directly. The user doesn't even need to know who you are—the problem is already solved.

An analogy: GEO is AI giving you an ad. AIO is AI doing business for you.

Big difference? Very big.

GEO's endpoint is "being seen." AIO's endpoint is "being used."

---

My friend said, for AI to call you, you first need to become "machine-readable."

What does machine-readable mean?

Today's websites are built for humans. Beautiful images, carefully designed layouts, marketing copy. But AI can't read these. It only recognizes structured data—API docs, JSON-LD, MCP interfaces.

You need to package your service into something AI can understand. Not "we provide professional solutions," but "input A, output B, price C."

The clearer, the easier to call.

I said, isn't this just making APIs?

He said, yes, but it's not just a technical problem. There's another issue called semantic alignment.

What's semantic alignment?

It's how you describe yourself. If you say "empowering enterprise digital transformation," AI doesn't know what you're talking about. If you say "providing enterprise cloud storage, 99.9% availability," AI knows exactly what you do.

Don't use marketing jargon. Use language AI can understand.

---

By the end, my friend mentioned something more interesting.

He said, eventually AI will spend money on its own.

I asked what that means.

He said, Silicon Valley is already researching AI wallets. An AI Agent that helps users book flights, buy services, pay bills. It judges for itself which service is good, which is cheap, which is reliable, then completes the payment on its own.

Traditional payment methods—filling in credit cards, billing addresses—are too cumbersome for AI. It needs one-click completion. Cryptocurrencies, Lightning Network—these are naturally suited for AI.

I said, isn't this a bit far from us?

He said, not far. If this trend takes off next year, those who position themselves this year have first-mover advantage. By the time everyone realizes, you're already late.

---

I've been thinking about this since I came back.

My previous mindset was: get more people to see me, get AI to mention me.

But maybe the next phase of competition isn't about being seen—it's about being called.

Is your service AI-friendly? Is your pricing transparent? Is your quality verifiable?

These used to be pitched to humans. In the future, they might be pitched to AI.

Users won't ask "why did you pick this one?" AI will judge for itself.

And we might not even know AI was ever here.
